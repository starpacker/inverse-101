# Light Field Microscope

> Reconstruct a 3D fluorescent volume from a single 2D light-field capture using a wave-optics forward model and 3D Richardson-Lucy deconvolution, where one sensor image must decode depth-dependent aliasing and axial cross-talk.

> Domain: Biology | Keywords: wave imaging, light field imaging, deconvolution | Difficulty: Hard

## Background

Light-field microscopy places a microlens array (MLA) at the microscope image plane so that one camera exposure records both spatial and angular information. Each lenslet creates a local sub-image on the sensor, and the full sensor mosaic is interpreted as a multiplexed 4D light field. This permits snapshot volumetric imaging, but it trades lateral resolution for angular sampling.

The reference paper for this task is Broxton et al., *Wave optics theory and 3-D deconvolution for the light field microscope* (2013). Their key point is that the recorded light field should be modeled with wave optics, not only with geometric refocusing, and then inverted as a 3D deconvolution problem. The paper explicitly distinguishes two settings:

1. The general problem is full volume reconstruction from one 2D light-field image.
2. A planar USAF target is a special validation case where the reconstructed volume is constrained to a single occupied z-plane.

This cleaned task follows that same distinction. The main benchmark uses four thickness-1 validation volumes with occupied slices at `0`, `-30`, `-50`, and `-70` `um` to mirror the planar-target experiment. It also includes a separate five-slice demonstration that reconstructs a volume on `[-100, -75, -50, -25, 0]` `um` for a target placed at `-50` `um`.

## Problem Description

The measurement model is a Poisson imaging system:

$$
\mathbf{m} \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(A\mathbf{v}),
$$

where $\mathbf{m}$ is the sensor pixel-count vector, $\mathbf{v}$ is the 3D fluorescence volume obtained by stacking all depth-plane voxels, and $A$ is the system matrix. Each column of $A$ is generated by simulating one fluorescent source voxel through the wave-optics model. For voxel $i$ and sensor pixel $j$, the corresponding matrix entry satisfies

$$
a_{ji} \propto \left|U_{\mathrm{sens}}(\mathbf{x}_j)\right|^2,
$$

so, after normalization, it acts as the probability that a photon emitted from voxel $i$ is detected at sensor pixel $j$.

The sensor field $U_{\mathrm{sens}}$ is computed in three steps for each source voxel at axial defocus $\Delta z$.

Step 1: Debye diffraction integral. A fluorescent point source displaced by $\Delta z$ from the native object plane produces a defocused wavefront at the MLA plane. Under the Debye approximation, the pre-MLA field amplitude at transverse position $\mathbf{x}$ is

$$
U^-_{\mathrm{mla}}(\mathbf{x}) \propto \int_0^\alpha
\sqrt{\cos\theta}(1+\cos\theta)\,
J_0(kr\sin\theta)\,
\exp\!\left(
i\frac{u}{2}\frac{\sin^2(\theta/2)}{\sin^2(\alpha/2)}
\right)\,
\sin\theta\,d\theta.
$$

Here:

- $k = 2\pi n/\lambda$ is the wavenumber, with emission wavelength $\lambda$ and refractive index $n$
- $\alpha = \arcsin(\mathrm{NA}/n)$ is the objective half-aperture angle
- $r = |\mathbf{x}|/M$ is the transverse object-plane radius, with $M$ the objective magnification
- $u = 4k\Delta z\sin^2(\alpha/2)$ is the Lommel defocus parameter
- $\sqrt{\cos\theta}(1+\cos\theta)$ is the aplanatic-objective apodization factor
- $J_0$ is the zeroth-order Bessel function of the first kind

At $\Delta z = 0$, the wavefront is most compact and Airy-like. As $|\Delta z|$ grows, the field broadens into a defocus ring that illuminates more neighboring lenslets.

Step 2: MLA transmittance. The microlens array is modeled as a tiled set of thin converging lenses with centers $\mathbf{l}_\ell$, pitch $p_{\mathrm{ml}}$, and focal length $f_m$. Its transmittance is

$$
T_{\mathrm{MLA}}(\mathbf{x}) =
\sum_\ell
\mathrm{rect}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{l}_\ell}{p_{\mathrm{ml}}}\right)
\exp\!\left(-\frac{i\pi}{\lambda f_m}|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{l}_\ell|^2\right).
$$

The post-MLA field is

$$
U^+_{\mathrm{mla}}(\mathbf{x}) =
U^-_{\mathrm{mla}}(\mathbf{x})\,T_{\mathrm{MLA}}(\mathbf{x}).
$$

Each lenslet captures only the portion of the incoming wavefront within its aperture and redirects it toward the sensor.

Step 3: Rayleigh-Sommerfeld propagation. The post-MLA field propagates a distance $f_m$ to the sensor through the angular-spectrum form of Rayleigh-Sommerfeld propagation:

$$
U_{\mathrm{sens}}(\mathbf{x}) =
\mathcal{F}^{-1}\!\left[
\hat{U}_{\mathrm{mla}}^{+}(\mathbf{k}_{\perp})
\exp\!\left(if_m\sqrt{k^2-|\mathbf{k}_{\perp}|^2}\right)
\right](\mathbf{x}).
$$

Here $\hat{U}_{\mathrm{mla}}^{+}$ is the 2D spatial Fourier transform of the post-MLA field and $\mathbf{k}_{\perp}$ is the transverse wavevector. Evanescent components with $|\mathbf{k}_{\perp}| > k$ are zeroed. The detected intensity pattern $|U_{\mathrm{sens}}(\mathbf{x})|^2$ then becomes one sparse column of the system matrix $A$.

Equivalently, in continuous notation the light-field image is the sum of all depth-dependent point-source responses:

$$
m(s, t) \sim \mathrm{Poisson}\!\left(
\iiint h(s, t; x, y, z)\,v(x, y, z)\,dx\,dy\,dz
\right).
$$

After discretization this becomes

$$
\mathbf{y} \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(H\mathbf{v}),
$$

where $H$ is the discretized PSF bank corresponding to $A$.

The task is therefore a 3D inverse problem: recover $\mathbf{v}$ from one 2D measurement $\mathbf{y}$. It is ill-posed because the number of sensor samples is limited, angular views alias together under each lenslet, and slices near the native object plane contain more redundant angular information than slices away from it.

The main benchmark uses the planar-target special case

$$
v(x, y, z) = x(x, y)\,\delta(z-z_0),
$$

so each evaluated example is still a volume reconstruction problem, but the depth axis has length `1`. The additional notebook demonstration removes that restriction and reconstructs a five-slice volume at `[-100, -75, -50, -25, 0]` `um`.

## Method Hints

Use the wave-optics model, not geometric refocusing, as the forward operator. The essential ingredients are the Debye objective PSF, the MLA phase modulation, and propagation from the MLA to the sensor. Once the PSF bank is assembled over a chosen depth grid, solve the inverse problem as a 3D deconvolution:

$$
\mathbf{v}^{(k+1)} =
\mathbf{v}^{(k)} \odot
\frac{H^\top\!\left(\mathbf{y} \oslash (H\mathbf{v}^{(k)} + \varepsilon)\right)}
{H^\top \mathbf{1}},
$$

where $\odot$ and $\oslash$ are elementwise multiplication and division, $\mathbf{y}$ is the measured light-field image, and $\varepsilon$ prevents division by zero.

For the planar-target benchmark, the reconstructed volume has one axial slice, so the same 3D algorithm reduces to a thickness-1 special case. The notebook's five-slice demo is the more general test: the target is placed at `-50` `um`, the reconstruction grid is `[-100, -75, -50, -25, 0]` `um`, and a correct reconstruction should concentrate most recovered energy at the `-50` `um` slice while leaving the off-target slices comparatively weak.

The natural baseline is a conventional microscope image formed with the same defocus. The scientific claim carried over from the paper is that wave-model 3D deconvolution improves optical sectioning away from the native object plane, especially in cases such as `-50` `um` and `-70` `um` where the conventional image is strongly blurred.
